AI-Guided Rapid Housing Pipeline

This page walks you, step by step, through getting help with housing when you are unsheltered or at risk, especially if you rely on daily medication (like HIV treatment). Each step has a ready-to-use prompt you can copy into an AI chat (such as ChatGPT) to draft emails, scripts, and documents for you.

Tip Tap “Copy prompt” under any step → paste into your AI chat → get custom letters and scripts instantly.

Phase 1

Document the crisis & your medication risk

Start here if you are unsheltered or about to lose housing.
Step 1
Write your crisis story once (so you don’t have to keep repeating it)
Crisis Snapshot

This creates a simple “Crisis Summary” you can reuse for every agency, worker, or intake form.

AI prompt to copy:
Help me write a short, clear Crisis Summary I can reuse with agencies. Ask me: - where I slept last night, - whether I have any place to sleep tonight, - what medication I need to take daily (for example, HIV or other serious conditions), - how long I’ve been in this situation, - what has already happened with hospitals, clinics, or shelters. Then, write a 1-page Crisis Summary in plain language that I can copy into emails and forms.
  • Answer the AI’s questions honestly (no need for perfect wording).
  • Save the Crisis Summary somewhere safe on your phone (Notes, Files, email to yourself).
  • Use the same crisis text again and again instead of starting over each time.
Why this matters: Having one clear written story saves your energy and creates a consistent record that shows this is an ongoing crisis, not a one-time event.
Step 2
Explain your medication risk (HIV, ART, or other daily treatment)
Medical Necessity

Housing programs and health systems move faster when they see the medical risk clearly.

AI prompt to copy:
Using my Crisis Summary, help me write a short “Medical Risk Statement” explaining: - that I am living with HIV or another serious condition, - that I need to take my medication every day (for example: Biktarvy or other ART), - why being homeless or unstable makes it hard or unsafe to keep my medication schedule, - how this could harm my health if it continues. Make it 1–2 paragraphs, in plain language, that I can attach to housing and health requests.
  • Copy the Medical Risk Statement into your notes or email.
  • Include it whenever you ask for a hotel voucher or emergency housing.
  • Tell workers: “This is medically necessary housing, not just a preference.”
Key point: HIV and other serious conditions are protected disabilities. Stabilizing your housing to protect treatment is a reasonable accommodation, not a favor.

Phase 2

Work with your case manager (or clinic social worker)

Use this if you have a case manager or clinic you work with.
Step 3
Send an urgent request for housing help
First Ask

This creates a focused email or message so your case manager knows it is urgent and medical.

AI prompt to copy:
Write an email to my case manager asking for immediate help with emergency housing or a hotel voucher. Use this structure: - Brief subject line: “Urgent: Medically Necessary Housing Help Needed” - First paragraph: one-sentence summary that I am homeless or about to be, and that I am living with HIV (or another serious condition) and need daily medication. - Second paragraph: paste or summarize my Crisis Summary. - Third paragraph: paste or summarize my Medical Risk Statement. - Final paragraph: clearly request a stabilization or hotel voucher referral today, ask when it will be submitted, and ask them to reply with confirmation. Use respectful but firm language. Keep it simple and clear.
  • Copy and send the email to your case manager (or clinic social worker).
  • Take a screenshot after sending, for your records.
  • Note the date and time you sent it.
Step 4
Follow up if no one responds
24–48 Hour Rule

If they do not reply, you need a written follow-up to show you tried.

AI prompt to copy:
Write a short follow-up email to my case manager. Explain: - I sent an urgent housing request on [DATE], - I am still unsheltered or at risk, - I need to know whether a referral for emergency housing or hotel voucher has been submitted, - I am asking for a quick written update today. Keep it calm but firm. Mention that this is about my ability to keep taking my medication safely.
  • Send this follow-up if you have no answer after about 24 hours.
  • If there is still no response after 48 hours, move to the County step in the next phase.
Why follow-ups matter: Written follow-ups prove you asked for help and didn’t simply “drop out.” That evidence is powerful if you later need oversight or legal help.

Phase 3

Bring the County into the loop

Use if your clinic or case manager is slow or unresponsive.
Step 5
Ask the County to help move things forward
County Engagement

The County often funds or oversees the programs your clinic uses. They can push for action.

AI prompt to copy:
Help me write an email to my County HIV/housing program. Include: - A short version of my Crisis Summary, - A short version of my Medical Risk Statement, - The dates I asked my case manager for help, - That I have not received timely housing support. Ask the County to: - Confirm they received this message, - Help coordinate with my case manager and any HOPWA or hotel voucher program, - Treat this as medically necessary stabilization housing, not just a routine request.
  • Send this to the County HIV program or housing email you have.
  • Call if possible and say: “I also emailed a summary of my situation.”

Phase 4

When delays are dangerous: oversight & civil rights

Use this if you’ve been trying and nothing moves.
Step 6
Prepare oversight letters (HUD, Joint Commission)
Oversight

This turns “I’m struggling” into “Systems may be failing a protected person.”

AI prompt to copy:
Using my Crisis Summary and Medical Risk Statement, help me draft: 1) A short letter to HUD describing: - that I am living with HIV or another serious condition, - that I am unsheltered or at high risk, - that I requested HOPWA or similar housing help, - that delays are putting my treatment at risk. 2) A similar letter to The Joint Commission describing: - that my health care system knows I am unsheltered and medically vulnerable, - that I have not received timely help with stabilization housing, - that this creates a patient safety and continuity-of-care risk. Make both letters respectful but firm, and in plain language.
Step 7
Prepare or update an OCR civil rights complaint
Civil Rights

OCR enforces civil rights when people with disabilities are blocked or harmed by systems.

AI prompt to copy:
Help me write text I can use for an HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) complaint. Include: - that I am a person living with HIV or another serious medical condition, - that my condition is a disability under Federal law (ADA / Section 504 / Section 1557), - that I am unsheltered or unstable and this is harming or threatening my treatment, - that I asked my providers and local programs for help and experienced serious delay or denial, - that this has caused risk or harm. Write it in clear paragraphs I can paste into the OCR complaint form.
  • Go to the OCR online complaint form and paste the text the AI creates.
  • Keep your OCR confirmation number somewhere safe.
Impact over intent: OCR focuses on what happened to you (impact), not whether people “meant” to discriminate. Your written record is what matters.

Phase 5

Daily crisis loop – what to do each day until housed

Repeat daily until you have safe, stable housing.
Step 8
Ask: “What should I do today?”
Daily Checklist

Use this each morning to keep things moving, even when you’re exhausted.

AI prompt to copy:
Based on the crisis situation and steps I’ve taken so far, help me create a simple “today” checklist. Include: - who I should follow up with (case manager, County, housing agency), - which emails or calls are overdue, - whether I should send an escalation or reminder, - what to note in my communication log today, - one small self-care or safety step I can take. Make it brief and numbered so I can follow it step by step.
Status Helper Use 1 step at a time – you do not need to do everything in one day. You can reuse these prompts as often as needed.
This guide is informational only and does not replace legal or medical advice. Use it to help you speak up, get support, and keep your treatment safe while you seek housing.
